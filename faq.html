Buying a Home

How do I know if now is a good time to buy in Central Oregon?

The honest answer: it depends on your situation more than the market. If you plan to own for 5+ years, have stable income, and the monthly payment fits comfortably, timing the market matters less than time in the market. I'm happy to walk through current conditions in Bend, Redmond, or wherever you're looking. Find out what you can afford with the Home Affordability Calculator on this site, it does this math instantly with your real figures.

What's the difference between pre-qualified and pre-approved?

Pre-qualification is a quick estimate based on what you tell a lender. Pre-approval means the lender has verified your income, credit, and assets. In Central Oregon's market, sellers take pre-approved buyers far more seriously — get pre-approved before we start touring homes.

How much do I really need for a down payment?

Less than most people think. Conventional loans go as low as 3–5% down, FHA is 3.5%, and VA/USDA can be 0% for those who qualify. 20% down avoids mortgage insurance, but waiting years to save 20% often costs more than the insurance would. We'll run your specific numbers.

What's earnest money and how much should I offer?

Earnest money is a good-faith deposit that shows the seller you're serious — typically 1–2% of the purchase price here. It's held in escrow and applies toward your purchase at closing. With proper contingencies, it's protected if the deal falls through for covered reasons.

How long does the buying process take?

Once you're under contract, plan on 30–45 days to close with financing. The search itself varies — some clients find the right home in two weeks, others take six months. I'd rather you take the time to buy the right property than rush into the wrong one.

Do I need a buyer's agent? What does it cost me?

You're not required to have one, but going unrepresented in a significant negotiation rarely works in your favor. Buyer agent compensation is negotiable and we'll discuss it transparently up front — in many transactions the seller offers compensation, and we'll cover exactly how it works before you sign anything.

What should I look for in a home inspection?

Focus on the expensive systems: roof, foundation, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, and drainage. Cosmetic issues are negotiating points; structural issues are decision points. Having renovated properties myself, I often spot concerns during showings — before you spend money on an inspection.

Selling Your Home

How do you determine what my home is worth?

I run a comparative market analysis using recent sales of genuinely similar properties, then adjust for condition, location, lot, and upgrades. I'll also tell you what I think the market will bear right now — even if it's not the number you were hoping for. Honest pricing nets more than wishful pricing.

Should I renovate before listing or sell as-is?

It depends on the renovation. Paint, flooring, and lighting usually return more than they cost. Full kitchen remodels before selling usually don't. As someone who renovates property, I'll give you a room-by-room read on what's worth doing and what isn't.

What are closing costs and who pays them?

Closing costs include title insurance, escrow fees, prorated taxes, and lender fees. In Oregon, sellers typically pay for the owner's title policy and half of escrow; buyers pay lender costs and their half of escrow. Everything is negotiable in the offer. I'll prepare a net sheet so you know your actual proceeds before you accept anything.

How long does it take to sell in Central Oregon?

Well-priced, well-presented homes in Bend and Redmond often go under contract within a few weeks. Overpriced homes sit — and sitting costs you negotiating leverage. Pricing strategy matters more than season.

What is a seller's net sheet?

A line-item estimate of what you'll actually walk away with after your mortgage payoff, commissions, closing costs, and prorations. I prepare one before we list and update it with every offer, so you're always comparing real numbers — not just sale prices.

Investing

What's a good first investment property?

For most people: something simple, rentable, and forgiving. A solid single-family home or duplex in a strong rental area, without major deferred maintenance. Your first deal teaches you how to operate — it doesn't need to be a home run, it needs to not be a disaster.

What is house hacking?

Living in a property while renting out part of it — the other unit of a duplex, a basement apartment, an ADU, or spare rooms. Your tenants offset your mortgage, you qualify for owner-occupant financing (lower down payments), and you learn landlording with training wheels. It's one of the most accessible ways to start investing.

How do I analyze if a rental property is a good deal?

Start with cash flow: rent minus ALL expenses (mortgage, taxes, insurance, maintenance, vacancy, management). Then look at cash-on-cash return, appreciation potential, and value-add opportunities. Try the calculators on this site — then let's pressure-test the assumptions together on a real property.

What is a cap rate? What's a good one for Central Oregon?

Cap rate is net operating income divided by purchase price — the return if you paid all cash. Central Oregon cap rates are typically modest compared to Midwest markets because buyers also pay for appreciation, lifestyle demand, and rental strength. Here, the total return story matters more than the cap rate alone.

Should I buy a duplex or a single-family rental?

Duplexes usually cash flow better and one vacancy doesn't zero your income. Single-family homes usually appreciate better and attract longer-term tenants. The right answer depends on your goals, capital, and how hands-on you want to be — this is one of my favorite conversations to have with clients.

What's the BRRRR strategy?

Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat. You buy a property below market value, renovate it, rent it out, then refinance based on the new higher value to pull your capital back out — and use it for the next deal. It works, but it depends on buying right and renovating on budget. Both are where experience matters.

How do I get started investing with limited capital?

House hacking is usually the answer — owner-occupant loans allow 3.5–5% down on properties with up to four units. Other paths include partnering, seller financing, and buying a fixer-upper where sweat equity replaces capital. Limited money is a constraint, not a wall.

General Real Estate

What's the difference between a broker and an agent?

In Oregon, all licensees are technically brokers — there's no separate "agent" license. A principal broker has additional experience and licensing and can supervise other brokers. Practically, what matters most is your individual broker's experience and judgment.

What is earnest money and when do I get it back?

It's your good-faith deposit, held in escrow. You get it back if the transaction fails for a reason covered by your contingencies — inspection, financing, appraisal. If you simply walk away without a covered reason, the seller may keep it. Contingencies exist to protect you; we'll structure them carefully.

What is a 1031 exchange?

A tax-deferral tool that lets you sell an investment property and roll the proceeds into another investment property without paying capital gains tax now. Strict timelines apply (45 days to identify, 180 to close), and you'll need a qualified intermediary. I can coordinate the real estate side and connect you with the right professionals.

What does "as-is" mean in real estate?

The seller won't make repairs — what you see is what you get. It does NOT mean you can't inspect; you can and absolutely should. "As-is" listings are often where value-add opportunity hides, if you can accurately estimate the repair costs. That's where my renovation background earns its keep.
